ZFS FIlesystem (RAID & RAIDZ)

* Features
   Pooled storage, copy-on-write, snapshots, data integrity verification & automatic repair, RAID-Z, max 16 Exobytes file size,
   maximum 256 quadrillion Zetabytes storage

* Pooled storage
   -ZFS combines features of a file system & a volume manager

   -can create a filesystem than spans across a series of drives (or a pool)

* Copy-on-write
   -on most filesystems, when data is overwritten, its lost forever
   
   -on zfs, the new info is written top a different block
   -when write is complete, filesystem's medata is updated to point to new info 
   -if system crashes during the write, the old data is preserved. no need to fsck after a system crash
  
* Snapshots
   -ZFS uses snapshots to track changes in filesystem & live system contains changes made since the snapshot was taken
   -as new data is written to live filesystem, new blocks are allocted to store this data
   -designed to track changes to files, but not the addition & creation of files
   -can rollback live system to a previous snapshot (all changes made since snapshot will be lost)
   
* Data integrity verification & automatic repair
   -whenever new dadta is written to zfs, it creates a checksum. when data is read, the checksum is verified. if the checksum doesn't 
      match, zfs knows an error happened. zfs will auotmatically attempt to correct the error.

*RAID-Z
   -zfs ha sits own implementation of RAID
   -RAID-Z is a variant of RAID-5
   -designed to overcome RAID-5's write hole error "in which the data & parity information become inconsistent after an unexpected restart.

   -parity: parity computations used in RAID drive arrays for fault tolerance by alculaating teh data in 2 drives & storing the result on the 3rd.
      After a failed drive is replaced, the RAID controller rebuilds the lost data from the other 2 drives. RAID filesystems often have a "hot" spare 
      drive ready & waiting to replace a drive that fails.

* RAID-Z levels
   -RAIDZ-1: minimum # of drives for RAIDZ1 is 2 (best practice use "power of two plus parity")

      Ex:
         three(2+1), five(3+2), or 9(8+1) disks
    -RAIDZ-2 & RAIDZ-3
      RAIDZ2: should use four(2+2), six(4+2), ten(8+2) or eighteen(16+2) drives
      
      RAIDZ-3: five(2+3), eleven(8+3), or nineteen(16+3) disks

   1) filesystem: a directory tree
   2) volume (zvol): a volume represented as a block device
   3) snapshot: snapshot of a filesystem
   4) bookmark: A snapshot that does not hold data - used for incremental reprication
   
   -RAIDZ-2 requires at least 2 drives for storage & 2 for parity
   -RAIDZ-3 requires at least 2 drives for storage & 3 for parity

   -when drives are added to the RAIDZ pools, have to be added in multiples of 2

 * Huge storage potential
    -zfs creators decided to use 128 bit 
    -offering 16 billion billion times the capicity of 32 or 64 bit systems

* RAID & RAIDZ
   -only minor difference between them
   -functionally identical

   -uses smaller RAIDS in partitions called UDEVS (virtual devices)
   -joining multiple UDEVS makes a zpool

   RAID0: also called striping
      -spreads across multiple drives to get the added speed of them all reading & writing together, best performing, most space, efficient RAID level, but gives no
      data security. In a drive failure, your whole storage pool could be lost
 
      -zfs calls this a striped UDEV & has slightly more protection against silent data corruption than standard RAID0 due to its self-healing with checksums.

      -not good for anyone not wanting big risk of losing all their data

   RAID1:
      -mirrors data between drives to essentially have a local backup of all your data

      -in zfs this would be done by mirroring between UDEVS (collection of block devices or character devices - usually disks & drives - arranged in a particular topology   
      -with zfs can mirror udevs as many times as you want to help protect against drive failure
      -results in more wasted space for extra protection of an extra drive failure per mirror

      -RAID0 is often used in conjunction with other RAID levels for boot drives as its space efficiency is worse than the parity RAID levels

   RAID5:
       -in zfs RAID5 is called RAIDZ-1  
        RAID uses a prity block which gives it the ability to rebuild the lost data if a drive fails

       -performs same data striping across drives for the added performance benefit of RAID0
       -data striping: splits data logically across multiple physical storage devices
       -used to increase the speed at which data can be read & written 
       -not limited by the speed of a dedicated parity drive
       *one of the most popular raid levels because it combines performance & data security
       -RAID5 protects your data  against 1 drive failure, but if another goes out before rebuilding it the whole pool could be lost
       -RAID-Z has benefit over RAID5 as it solved the write-hole phenomenon that usually plagues parity & striping raid levels

   RAID6:
      -simular to RAID5 but 2 drives for parity
      -zfs equivalent to RAIDZ-2
      -fairly safe RAID level since it can withstand 2 drive failures & still rebuild without losing pool
      -RAIDZ-2 doesn't face write-hole phenomenon like RAID6

   RAID10:
      -is just mirroring & striping used together, then across the top, each mirrored pair gets striped across the whole set to create a single virtual drive
      -has redundancy benefit of RAID1 & performance of RAID0
      -the only way RAID10 lacks in is efficient use of its capacity. all data is stored twice, so half the effective capacity of RAID0
      -RAID10 is simply striping mirrored UDEVS

   RAID01: same as RAID10 but backwards & worse
      -stripes data across pools of disks & then mirrors thos pools 
      -performance is identical to RAID10 but chances of losing pool is higher
      -increase chance of drive failure when rebuilding
      -as you add more disks in RAID01 chance of losing your storage with 2 disk failures is never better than 50%
      -most drives you add closer to 0%
      *RAID01 is NEVER used over RAID10 anymore

   RAID50:
      -essentially several seperate RAID5 pools (with a drive for parity in each) & RAID0 added across the top of them, striping data across the pools.
      -can withstand 1 drive failure for each of the RAID5 pools you have
      -much higher redundancy than just RAID5, but less data effifient
      -need a drive of parity per pool
      -whole pool can still fail. failure if 2 drives from any of the RAID5's pools go down.
      -decreases capacity since it uses extra parity blcks for each RAID5 pool
      -in zfs this would be done by creating multiple volumes of RAIDZ-1 & striping them across each other

   RAID60:
      -simular to RAID50, uses multiple RAID6 pools striped across each other
      -uses a lot of capacity. each RAID6 requires 2 drives worth of parity
      -huge amount of redundancy
      -2 drives can fail out of any of the RAID6 polls & the total pool can still be rebuilt
      -as long as 3 drives from the same pool don't fail, the data can still be rebuilt
      -huge insulation against drive failures
      -in zfs RAID60 is equivalent of multiple RAIDZ-2 pools striped across each other
      -very space inefficient option
      -biggest issue some may experience with "stripe  & parity" raid levels (RAID5, RAID6, RAID50 & RAID60) is write-hole phenomenon
      -if power is lost during a write, the data is corrupted & cannot be rebuilt
      -RAIDZ doen't have this problem

   *RAID & RAIDZ
      -RAIDZ has slower reads for its checksum, limited to the speed of 1 drive
      -slows down when reading chunks of data at random in RAIDZ
      -RAID50 & RAID60 are tailored to enterprise envirornments where data integrity & performance are crucial. (servers, databases & virtualized envirornments)
